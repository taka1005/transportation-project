\begin{abstract}
\noindent We test how two Boston mobility systems depart from Poisson arrival assumptions and quantify the resulting error in queueing predictions. Using four months of data from Bluebikes at Kendall/MIT and MIT Vassar~St (user-driven, finite-capacity) and the MBTA Red Line at Kendall/MIT (schedule-driven), we reject exponential inter-arrival times for every system ($p \approx 0$ under Kolmogorov--Smirnov, Anderson--Darling, and $\chi^2$) but in opposite directions: Bluebikes is overdispersed ($\mathrm{CV} = 1.75$--$1.90$, best fit Weibull) and MBTA is underdispersed ($\mathrm{CV} = 0.63$--$0.71$, best fit log-normal). Analytical Poisson queueing underestimates Bluebikes wait by roughly an order of magnitude and Erlang~B blocking at Kendall/MIT by 55-fold, while overestimating MBTA wait by 5--12$\times$. The 55$\times$ blocking gap persists even when the simulation uses empirical arrivals, suggesting service-process non-stationarity (and untested arrival non-stationarity) as a separate error source that stationary arrival-process correction alone cannot address.
\end{abstract}

% ===== 1. Introduction =====
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Queueing theory in transportation is built on the Poisson arrival assumption. Closed-form results for the M/M/$c$ and M/M/$c/c$ models \cite{kleinrock1975,erlang1917} give analysts tractable estimates of wait time and blocking probability. These models inform some operational decisions directly --- bike-share dock counts and rebalancing cadence are a clear case --- and provide back-of-envelope wait estimates for studies of transit user experience and transfer planning, while transit infrastructure sizing is governed by demand forecasts and operating constraints rather than M/M/1 wait. Real urban mobility arrivals are shaped by timetables, time-of-day demand, and disruptions, so the assumption is approximate. The practical question is not whether arrivals are Poisson --- they almost never are --- but how much queueing predictions suffer when the assumption fails, and whether the residual error matters for the decision the prediction feeds.

We study two Boston systems whose arrival processes are structurally opposite. Bluebikes is a user-driven bike-share with finite-capacity docks; the MBTA Red Line at Kendall/MIT runs a published timetable on an effectively uncapacitated platform. We ask: (1)~how far do the observed arrivals deviate from Poisson; (2)~how accurately do Poisson-based models predict wait and blocking; and (3)~under what conditions is Poisson a defensible engineering approximation?

Our contributions are: quantitative Poisson-deviation measurements for both systems; a side-by-side comparison of analytical Poisson queueing against discrete-event simulation driven by the empirical and best-fit arrival distributions; identification of a 55$\times$ Erlang~B blocking gap at Kendall/MIT that stationary arrival-process correction alone cannot close, suggesting non-stationarity in $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$ as a distinct error source; and a decision-threshold framework that evaluates Poisson's error by \emph{direction}, \emph{magnitude}, and whether the prediction is on the decision path at all --- the user-side blocking under-prediction is binding, while the MBTA wait over-prediction is large in relative terms but does not feed any operational decision.

% ===== 2. Data =====
\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

\paragraph{Bluebikes.} Public trip data, September--December 2025, at M32004 Kendall/MIT (23 docks, transit hub) and M32042 MIT Vassar~St (53 docks, campus dormitory area). An arrival is a trip-end timestamp. After operating-hours restriction, disruption-day exclusion, and fullness exclusion (below), $N = 9{,}566$ (Kendall/MIT) and $19{,}300$ (MIT Vassar).

\paragraph{MBTA Red Line.} LAMP performance data \cite{mbta_lamp} for Kendall/MIT (\texttt{place-knncl}) in the same window, Northbound ($N = 20{,}366$) and Southbound ($N = 20{,}880$). An arrival is a train-at-platform event. Overnight gaps are excluded.

\paragraph{Fullness exclusion.} Observed Bluebikes arrivals are censored when a station is at capacity. We reconstruct station inventory by forward event replay with bookkeeping clamps for unlogged operator rebalancing, and flag a full-capacity interval only when a natural $C_s-1 \to C_s$ trip-end transition is followed by a trip-start with no intervening trip-end; IATs overlapping any committed interval are excluded from $\lambda$, $\mu$, and distribution fitting. Kendall/MIT is full $3.73\%$ of operating time and MIT Vassar~St $2.88\%$; we use these as the validation target for Erlang~B blocking (\S\ref{sec:results-erlangb}). Algorithm in Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Code, derived data, and figures at \url{https://github.com/taka1005/transportation-project} (MIT license).

% ===== 3. Methods =====
\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Arrival-process characterization.} For each system we compute the mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation ($\mathrm{CV} = \sigma/\mu$), and skewness of the inter-arrival time, and the index of dispersion ($\mathrm{IoD} = \mathrm{Var}[N]/\mathbb{E}[N]$) of arrival counts in 15-, 30-, and 60-minute windows. $\mathrm{CV} = \mathrm{IoD} = 1$ are the Poisson values. We fit exponential, log-normal, Weibull, and gamma by maximum likelihood and rank them by AIC. Fits are tested with Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS), Anderson--Darling (AD), and Pearson $\chi^2$ statistics.

\paragraph{Queueing models.} Bluebikes: M/M/$c$ (infinite-queue idealisation; real stations reject rather than queue) with $c$ the dock count,
\begin{equation}
W_q = \frac{C(c, a)}{c\mu - \lambda}, \quad a = \lambda/\mu,\ \rho = a/c,
\label{eq:mmc}
\end{equation}
where $C(c,a)$ is the Erlang~C probability of queueing, and M/M/$c/c$ giving the Erlang~B blocking $B(c,a) = a\,B(c-1,a) / (c + a\,B(c-1,a))$ with $B(0,a) = 1$. MBTA: M/M/1 with service = dwell time. Bluebikes $\mu$ is estimated via Little's Law \cite{little1961} from the average non-full dock occupancy. Derivations in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\paragraph{Discrete-event simulation.} We implement each system in SimPy \cite{simpy} with three arrival generators: Poisson ($\mathrm{Exp}(1/\lambda)$), empirical i.i.d.\ bootstrap from the observed IATs, and best-fit sampling from the MLE Weibull (Bluebikes) or log-normal (MBTA). Each configuration uses 20,000 arrivals with a 2,000-arrival warmup and 10 replications (95\% CI via Student's~$t$).

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Arrivals reject Poisson in opposite directions}\label{sec:results-arrival}

Every system rejects exponential inter-arrivals at $p \approx 0$ under KS, AD, and $\chi^2$ (Appendix~\ref{app:gof}). Table~\ref{tab:summary} summarises the direction: Bluebikes is overdispersed ($\mathrm{CV}$ 1.75--1.90, IoD 2--8); MBTA is underdispersed ($\mathrm{CV}$ 0.63--0.71, IoD 0.4--0.6). The best parametric fit is Weibull for Bluebikes (shape $c \approx 0.72$, monotone-decreasing hazard) and log-normal for MBTA (shape $s \approx 0.5$), with AIC improvements of $2{,}167$ to $17{,}833$ over exponential. MBTA in fact departs from exponential \emph{farther} in KS distance (0.27--0.29) than Bluebikes (0.13--0.14): regularity is as extreme a Poisson violation as burstiness.

Within-day segmentation (Appendix~\ref{app:hour}, Table~\ref{tab:cv-hour}; empirical CDFs with fits in Appendix~\ref{app:figures}, Fig.~\ref{fig:cdfs}) attributes the Bluebikes CV to non-stationarity: Kendall/MIT falls to $\mathrm{CV} \approx 1.0$ at midnight and peaks near 2.0; MBTA stays below~1 at every hour. A single-window Poisson hypothesis is thus rejected \emph{more} strongly at peak hours, not less.

At Kendall/MIT Poisson M/M/$c$ predicts $W_q = 0.8$~s against empirical $9.4$~s --- about an order-of-magnitude underestimate. For MBTA the sign flips: 20.1~s vs 4.2~s Northbound (5$\times$ over), 10.7~s vs 0.9~s Southbound (12$\times$ over). The Northbound--Southbound spread is driven by dwell-time asymmetry at Kendall/MIT (mean dwell 82~s vs 61~s, reflecting heavier morning-peak boardings on the inbound platform), not by arrival-process differences. Best-fit-DES tracks empirical-DES to within a second everywhere, so a Weibull or log-normal fit retains enough of the arrival-process information to reproduce the queueing outcome. MIT Vassar~St is a $W_q$ null case under every model (53 docks drive the queueing wait to zero), yet \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} shows it is \emph{not} a blocking null case once the observed fullness rate is reconstructed properly.

\subsection{The Erlang B blocking gap points to non-stationarity}\label{sec:results-erlangb}

At Kendall/MIT the Erlang~B prediction for blocking is $0.068\%$ against an observed full-capacity rate of $3.73\%$: a $55\times$ gap. Finite-capacity DES with \emph{empirical} IATs gives $0.54\%$, closing only about a seventh of the gap. At MIT Vassar~St the gap is qualitatively similar (Erlang~B $\approx 0\%$ vs observed $2.88\%$; empirical-DES produces no blocking events). Stationary arrival correction alone therefore cannot explain the observed blocking; the residual is consistent with time-varying $\mu(t)$ (dock occupancy swells at peak departures, collapses midday) and with time-varying $\lambda(t)$ that the i.i.d.\ bootstrap does not test. Neither source is captured by an M/M/$c/c$ model or by empirical-i.i.d.-with-exponential-service DES. The MBTA has no analogue at $\rho = 0.15$--$0.20$.

% ===== 5. Discussion =====
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

Poisson's error has three orthogonal axes: \emph{direction} (over- vs.\ under-estimation), \emph{magnitude relative to the decision threshold}, and \emph{whether the prediction is on the decision path at all}. The two systems we study fall into qualitatively different regimes.

\paragraph{User-side, on the decision path: Bluebikes blocking.} Poisson under-estimates Kendall/MIT blocking by 55-fold. Bike-share planners do use queueing-style reasoning to size docks and tune rebalancing cadence, so a Poisson target would miss service failures by an order of magnitude; users find full stations and abandon trips. Bursty arrivals (CV 1.75--1.90) average away the tail events that drive blocking. This is the binding error in our study.

\paragraph{Off the decision path: MBTA wait.} MBTA at $\rho = 0.15$--$0.20$: Poisson overestimates $W_q$ by 5--12$\times$. The relative error is large, but transit infrastructure is not sized from per-train $W_q$: platform length is inherited capital, service frequency is set from demand and operations constraints, and crowding (not queueing wait) is the customer metric. 20~s vs 4~s are both below the minute-scale resolution of any transit decision.

\paragraph{Error direction is predictable from structure.} User-driven, unscheduled systems produce overdispersed arrivals and are under-predicted by Poisson; timetabled systems produce underdispersed arrivals and are over-predicted. Evaluate direction first, then magnitude against the specific decision.

% ===== 6. Limitations =====
\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Latent demand suppression at Bluebikes.} Full-capacity IATs are excluded from $\lambda$ and distribution fitting (\S\ref{sec:data}, Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}), so $\lambda$ is unbiased for realised trips; the residual is latent demand from users who divert or abandon when expecting a full station and never appear in the dataset. Mellou and Jaillet~\cite{mellou2019} give a lost-demand estimator targeting this residual; substituting estimated arrivals would confound a real Poisson deviation with an estimator artefact.

\paragraph{DES destroys inter-arrival autocorrelation.} Empirical-DES samples IATs i.i.d., preserving the marginal distribution but not the sequential structure imposed by the timetable. This is why MBTA Southbound empirical-DES reports $W_q = 0.9$~s rather than essentially zero.

\paragraph{Scope.} Two Bluebikes stations, one MBTA station, four months. The structural argument should generalise; specific magnitudes should not.

\paragraph{Non-stationarity unmodelled.} The $55\times$ residual is consistent with time-varying $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$; the stationary i.i.d.\ bootstrap of \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} tests neither, so the attribution to service is suggestive.

\paragraph{Inventory reconstruction uncertainty.} $I_s$ carries a bounded rebalancing bias between clamps; our retract rule (Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}) biases toward omitting short full spans, so observed fullness and the $55\times$ gap are conservative lower bounds.

% ===== 7. Future Work =====
\section{Future Work}\label{sec:future}

Three extensions target the residual $55\times$ gap. (1)~Re-running \S\ref{sec:results-arrival} and \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} on arrivals reconstructed with the Mellou--Jaillet estimator~\cite{mellou2019} would separate censoring-induced from real non-Poissonness. (2)~Pairing a non-homogeneous Poisson generator (hour-of-day $\lambda(t)$) with time-varying $\mu(t)$ for Bluebikes would test whether joint non-stationarity closes the gap; the i.i.d.\ bootstrap of \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} captures neither. (3)~For MBTA, an autocorrelation-preserving generator (time-series bootstrap or Markov-modulated Poisson) should drive empirical-DES $W_q$ toward zero.

% ===== 8. Conclusion =====
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Poisson is good enough only when its error is small relative to the decision it feeds, and only when the prediction is on a decision path at all. Bluebikes under-estimates blocking by $55\times$ and feeds dock-count and rebalancing choices, so the error is binding; MBTA over-estimates wait by 5--12$\times$ but per-train $W_q$ is not how transit is planned, so the same magnitude is harmless. The Bluebikes gap persists under empirical-i.i.d.\ arrivals, leaving non-stationarity in $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$ as the residual; evaluate Poisson by direction, magnitude, and decision path.
