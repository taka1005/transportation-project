\begin{abstract}
\noindent We test how two Boston mobility systems depart from Poisson arrival assumptions and quantify the resulting error in queueing predictions. Using four months of data from Bluebikes at Kendall/MIT and MIT Vassar~St (user-driven, finite-capacity) and the MBTA Red Line at Kendall/MIT (schedule-driven), we reject exponential inter-arrival times for every system ($p \approx 0$ under Kolmogorov--Smirnov, Anderson--Darling, and $\chi^2$) but in opposite directions: Bluebikes is overdispersed ($\mathrm{CV} = 1.75$--$1.90$, best fit Weibull) and MBTA is underdispersed ($\mathrm{CV} = 0.63$--$0.71$, best fit log-normal). Analytical Poisson queueing underestimates Bluebikes wait by roughly an order of magnitude and Erlang~B blocking at Kendall/MIT by 55-fold, while overestimating MBTA wait by 5--12$\times$. Best-fit Weibull/log-normal IATs reproduce $W_q$ to within a second but close only about a seventh of the $55\times$ blocking gap: the marginal IAT distribution is sufficient for wait but not for blocking, and the residual is consistent with non-stationarity in $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$ that stationary arrival-process correction alone cannot address.
\end{abstract}

% ===== 1. Introduction =====
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Queueing theory in transportation is built on the Poisson arrival assumption. Closed-form results for the M/M/$c$ and M/M/$c/c$ models \cite{kleinrock1975,erlang1917} give analysts tractable estimates of wait time and blocking probability. These models inform some operational decisions directly --- bike-share dock counts and rebalancing cadence are a clear case --- while transit infrastructure sizing is governed by demand forecasts and operating constraints rather than M/M/1 wait. Real urban mobility arrivals are shaped by timetables, time-of-day demand, and disruptions, so the assumption is approximate. The practical question is not whether arrivals are Poisson --- they almost never are --- but how much queueing predictions suffer when the assumption fails, and whether the residual error matters for the decision the prediction feeds.

We study two Boston systems whose arrival processes are structurally opposite. Bluebikes is a user-driven bike-share with finite-capacity docks; the MBTA Red Line at Kendall/MIT runs a published timetable on an effectively uncapacitated platform. We ask: (1)~how far do the observed arrivals deviate from Poisson; (2)~how accurately do Poisson-based models predict wait and blocking; and (3)~under what conditions is Poisson a defensible engineering approximation?

Our contributions are: quantitative Poisson-deviation measurements for both systems; a side-by-side comparison of analytical Poisson queueing against discrete-event simulation driven by the empirical and best-fit arrival distributions; identification of a 55$\times$ Erlang~B blocking gap at Kendall/MIT that stationary arrival-process correction alone cannot close, suggesting non-stationarity in $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$ as a distinct error source; and a decision-threshold framework that evaluates Poisson's error by \emph{direction}, \emph{magnitude}, and whether the prediction is on the decision path at all --- the user-side blocking under-prediction is binding, while the MBTA wait over-prediction is large in relative terms but does not feed any operational decision.

% ===== 2. Data =====
\section{Data}\label{sec:data}

\paragraph{Bluebikes.} Public trip data, September--December 2025, at M32004 Kendall/MIT (23 docks, transit hub) and M32042 MIT Vassar~St (53 docks, campus dormitory area). An arrival is a trip-end timestamp. After operating-hours restriction, disruption-day exclusion, and fullness exclusion (below), $N = 9{,}566$ (Kendall/MIT) and $19{,}300$ (MIT Vassar).

\paragraph{MBTA Red Line.} LAMP performance data \cite{mbta_lamp} for Kendall/MIT (\texttt{place-knncl}) in the same window, Northbound ($N = 20{,}366$) and Southbound ($N = 20{,}880$). An arrival is a train-at-platform event. Overnight gaps are excluded.

\paragraph{Fullness exclusion.} Observed Bluebikes arrivals are censored when a station is at capacity. We reconstruct station inventory by forward event replay with bookkeeping clamps for unlogged operator rebalancing, and flag a full-capacity interval only when a natural $C_s-1 \to C_s$ trip-end transition is followed by a trip-start with no intervening trip-end; IATs overlapping any committed interval are excluded from $\lambda$, $\mu$, and distribution fitting. Kendall/MIT is full $3.73\%$ of operating time and MIT Vassar~St $2.88\%$; we use these as the validation target for Erlang~B blocking (\S\ref{sec:results-erlangb}). Algorithm in Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} Code, derived data, and figures at \url{https://github.com/taka1005/transportation-project} (MIT license).

% ===== 3. Methods =====
\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{Diagnostic intuition.} CV measures whether gaps between consecutive arrivals are more or less variable than exponential; IoD measures whether arrival counts in fixed time windows fluctuate more or less than a Poisson process. Segmenting both by hour, day-of-week, and peak/off-peak (Appendix~\ref{app:tables}) tests whether observed non-Poisson behavior is an aggregation artefact of mixing rate-heterogeneous periods or a persistent within-window feature.

\paragraph{Arrival-process characterization.} For each system we compute the mean, standard deviation, coefficient of variation ($\mathrm{CV} = \sigma/\mu$), and skewness of the inter-arrival time, and the index of dispersion ($\mathrm{IoD} = \mathrm{Var}[N]/\mathbb{E}[N]$) of arrival counts in 15-, 30-, and 60-minute windows. $\mathrm{CV} = \mathrm{IoD} = 1$ are the Poisson values. We fit exponential, log-normal, Weibull, and gamma by maximum likelihood and rank them by AIC. The four candidates span the relevant inter-arrival behaviors: exponential is the Poisson memoryless baseline; Weibull captures clustered or regular gaps through its shape parameter; log-normal arises naturally when arrivals are scheduled with multiplicative jitter; gamma is a flexible comparator that nests several of these regimes. Fits are tested with Kolmogorov--Smirnov (KS), Anderson--Darling (AD), and Pearson $\chi^2$ statistics. KS captures global CDF shape mismatch; AD weighs tail discrepancies, where rare events (including blocking) sit; $\chi^2$ provides a binned-frequency robustness check.

\paragraph{Queueing models.} Bluebikes: M/M/$c$ (infinite-queue idealisation; real stations reject rather than queue) with $c$ the dock count,
\begin{equation}
W_q = \frac{C(c, a)}{c\mu - \lambda}, \quad a = \lambda/\mu,\ \rho = a/c,
\label{eq:mmc}
\end{equation}
where $C(c,a)$ is the Erlang~C probability of queueing, and M/M/$c/c$ giving the Erlang~B blocking $B(c,a) = a\,B(c-1,a) / (c + a\,B(c-1,a))$ with $B(0,a) = 1$. MBTA: M/M/1 with service = dwell time. Bluebikes $\mu$ is estimated via Little's Law \cite{little1961} from the average non-full dock occupancy. Derivations in Appendix~\ref{app:derivations}.

\paragraph{Discrete-event simulation.} We implement each system in SimPy \cite{simpy} with three arrival generators: Poisson ($\mathrm{Exp}(1/\lambda)$), empirical i.i.d.\ bootstrap from the observed IATs, and best-fit sampling from the MLE Weibull (Bluebikes) or log-normal (MBTA). The empirical bootstrap preserves the marginal IAT distribution exactly but samples i.i.d., intentionally removing any sequential or time-of-day structure. We exploit this property in \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} to attribute residual blocking error to non-stationarity rather than to the marginal distribution. Each configuration uses 20,000 arrivals with a 2,000-arrival warmup and 10 replications (95\% CI via Student's~$t$).

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Arrivals reject Poisson in opposite directions}\label{sec:results-arrival}

Every system rejects exponential inter-arrivals at $p \approx 0$ under KS, AD, and $\chi^2$ (Appendix~\ref{app:gof}). Table~\ref{tab:summary} summarises the direction: Bluebikes is overdispersed ($\mathrm{CV}$ 1.75--1.90, IoD 3.2--4.7); MBTA is underdispersed ($\mathrm{CV}$ 0.63--0.71, IoD 0.52--0.58). The best parametric fit is Weibull for Bluebikes (shape $c \approx 0.72$, monotone-decreasing hazard) and log-normal for MBTA (shape $s \approx 0.5$), with AIC improvements of $2{,}167$ to $17{,}833$ over exponential. MBTA in fact departs from exponential \emph{farther} in KS distance (0.27--0.29) than Bluebikes (0.13--0.14): regularity is as extreme a Poisson violation as burstiness.

Within-day segmentation (Appendix~\ref{app:hour}, Table~\ref{tab:cv-hour}; empirical CDFs with fits in Appendix~\ref{app:figures}, Fig.~\ref{fig:cdfs}) attributes the Bluebikes CV to non-stationarity: Kendall/MIT falls to $\mathrm{CV} \approx 1.0$ at midnight and peaks near 2.0; MBTA stays below~1 at every hour. A single-window Poisson hypothesis is thus rejected \emph{more} strongly at peak hours, not less.

At Kendall/MIT Poisson M/M/$c$ predicts $W_q = 0.8$~s against empirical $9.4$~s --- about an order-of-magnitude underestimate. For MBTA the sign flips: 20.1~s vs 4.2~s Northbound (5$\times$ over), 10.7~s vs 0.9~s Southbound (12$\times$ over). The Northbound--Southbound spread is driven by dwell-time asymmetry at Kendall/MIT (mean dwell 82~s vs 61~s, reflecting heavier morning-peak boardings on the inbound platform), not by arrival-process differences. Best-fit-DES tracks empirical-DES to within a second everywhere, so a Weibull or log-normal fit retains enough of the arrival-process information to reproduce the queueing outcome. This conclusion does not survive the move from $W_q$ to blocking: \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} shows the same fit closes only a seventh of the $55\times$ Erlang~B gap, exposing non-stationarity as the dominant residual. MIT Vassar~St is a $W_q$ null case under every model, but \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} shows a non-trivial blocking gap there too.

\subsection{The Erlang B blocking gap points to non-stationarity}\label{sec:results-erlangb}

At Kendall/MIT the Erlang~B prediction for blocking is $0.068\%$ against an observed full-capacity rate of $3.73\%$: a $55\times$ gap. Finite-capacity DES with \emph{empirical} IATs gives $0.54\%$, closing only about a seventh of the gap. Best-fit Weibull DES gives $0.46\%$, comparable to empirical bootstrap and well short of the observed $3.73\%$; the marginal IAT distribution alone is insufficient regardless of whether resampled empirically or fit parametrically. At MIT Vassar~St the gap is qualitatively similar (Erlang~B $\approx 0\%$ vs observed $2.88\%$; empirical-DES produces no blocking events). Three time-structure effects neither stationary model captures: time-varying $\lambda(t)$ (bike returns peak at commute hours, averaged out by single-$\lambda$ models); time-varying $\mu(t)$ (Kendall dock turnover slows during peak return demand when rentals drop, missed by fixed-$\mu$ assumption); and temporal coupling between the two---$\lambda$ peaks coincide with $\mu$ valleys at the same hour, compounding blocking multiplicatively in a way neither stationary Erlang~B nor i.i.d.\ empirical DES exposes. The MBTA has no analogue at $\rho = 0.15$--$0.20$.

% ===== 5. Discussion =====
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

Two metric-level findings frame this discussion. For $W_q$, best-fit Weibull or log-normal samples match empirical-DES within a second across all four arrival processes --- the marginal IAT is sufficient. For blocking the same fit closes only a seventh of the $55\times$ Erlang~B gap at Kendall/MIT, hitting an i.i.d.\ ceiling. Whether Poisson is ``good enough'' depends on the metric as much as on the system.

Poisson's error has three orthogonal axes: \emph{direction} (over- vs.\ under-estimation), \emph{magnitude relative to the decision threshold}, and \emph{whether the prediction is on the decision path at all}. The two systems we study fall into qualitatively different regimes.

\paragraph{User-side, on the decision path: Bluebikes blocking.} Bike-share planners use queueing reasoning for dock counts and rebalancing cadence, so a Poisson target would miss service failures by an order of magnitude. This is the binding error in our study.

\paragraph{Off the decision path: MBTA wait.} MBTA at $\rho = 0.15$--$0.20$: Poisson overestimates $W_q$ by 5--12$\times$. The relative error is large, but per-train $W_q$ does not size transit infrastructure (platform length is inherited capital, service frequency comes from demand and operations), so the over-prediction is harmless rather than wasteful. Error \emph{direction} is predictable from structure: user-driven systems are overdispersed, timetabled systems underdispersed.

% ===== 6. Limitations and Future Work =====
\section{Limitations and future work}\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Latent demand suppression at Bluebikes.} Full-capacity IATs are excluded from $\lambda$ and distribution fitting (\S\ref{sec:data}, Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}), so $\lambda$ measures realised trips only; the residual is users who divert when expecting a full station. Mellou and Jaillet~\cite{mellou2019} give a lost-demand estimator targeting this residual; substituting estimated arrivals would confound a real Poisson deviation with an estimator artefact, but re-running \S\ref{sec:results-arrival} and \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} on Mellou--Jaillet-reconstructed arrivals would separate censoring-induced from real non-Poissonness.

\paragraph{DES destroys inter-arrival autocorrelation.} Empirical-DES samples IATs i.i.d., losing the timetable's sequential structure; this is why MBTA Southbound empirical-DES reports $W_q = 0.9$~s rather than zero. An autocorrelation-preserving generator (time-series bootstrap or Markov-modulated Poisson) should drive empirical-DES $W_q$ toward zero.

\paragraph{Non-stationarity unmodelled.} The $55\times$ residual is consistent with time-varying $\lambda(t)$ and/or $\mu(t)$; the stationary i.i.d.\ bootstrap of \S\ref{sec:results-erlangb} tests neither, so the attribution to service is suggestive. Pairing a non-homogeneous Poisson generator (hour-of-day $\lambda(t)$) with time-varying $\mu(t)$ for Bluebikes would test whether joint non-stationarity closes the gap.

\paragraph{Scope.} Two Bluebikes stations, one MBTA station, four months. The structural argument should generalise; specific magnitudes should not.

\paragraph{Inventory reconstruction uncertainty.} $I_s$ carries a bounded rebalancing bias between clamps; our retract rule (Appendix~\ref{app:fullness}) biases toward omitting short full spans, so observed fullness and the $55\times$ gap are conservative lower bounds.

% ===== 7. Conclusion =====
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Poisson is good enough only when its error is small relative to the decision it feeds, and only when the prediction is on a decision path at all. The Bluebikes blocking under-prediction is binding --- dock counts and rebalancing rely on queueing reasoning, and the 55-fold gap means Poisson misses service failures by an order of magnitude. The MBTA wait over-prediction is harmless because per-train $W_q$ does not feed transit planning.

For wait time the marginal IAT is enough: Weibull and log-normal reproduce empirical $W_q$ within a second. For blocking it is not --- empirical i.i.d.\ arrivals close only a seventh of the $55\times$ gap, leaving non-stationarity in $\lambda(t)$ and $\mu(t)$ as the residual that marginal correction cannot reach. Direction is predictable from structure: evaluate by direction, magnitude, and decision path.
